India's Healthcare Challenge

The Burden

India carries 20% of the world's disease burden with only 6.5% of global healthcare infrastructure. Northern India — the region GMCH primarily serves — accounts for disproportionate rates of neonatal mortality, cardiovascular disease, and trauma.

  • India loses 6.5 lakh newborns annually — preventable with NICU investment
  • 1 doctor per 1,511 people (WHO recommends 1:1,000)
  • 73% of India's rural population has no specialist access without travelling to a city hospital like GMCH
  • Punjab, Haryana & HP combined send over 2 lakh patients to Chandigarh annually

Why Government Hospitals Are the Answer

Private hospitals serve the top 15% of India's income pyramid. The other 85% depend entirely on institutions like GMCH. A single rupee invested in a government teaching hospital creates 8–12x the impact of the same rupee in a private facility.

  • Zero-cost care for Below Poverty Line families
  • Training 150 MBBS + 300+ PG doctors per year who serve rural India
  • Emergency care that does not turn away patients for inability to pay
  • Research that addresses Indian epidemiology, not Western disease profiles

About GMCH Chandigarh

History & Identity

Government Medical College & Hospital (GMCH), Sector 32, Chandigarh was established in 1991 under the Chandigarh Administration, affiliated with Panjab University. Over three decades, it has grown from a 500-bed facility into one of northern India's premier tertiary-care teaching hospitals, serving as the backbone of public healthcare for the tri-city region and beyond.

GMCH is not merely a hospital — it is a medical ecosystem: training the doctors who serve rural Punjab and Haryana, generating research that informs national health policy, and providing zero-cost specialist care to India's most vulnerable families.

India's highest accreditation grade — awarded to institutions with the strongest academic, clinical, and research standards.

Department Spotlight: Neonatology

The Department That Gave GMCH Its Voice in This Proposal

The Department of Neonatology at GMCH Chandigarh is led by Professor Suksham Jain, Head of Department — a nationally recognized specialist in neonatal intensive care, research, and medical education. Prof. Jain serves as Member Secretary of GMCH's Institutional Ethics Committee and has been a key figure in directing national-level research and governance in neonatal medicine.

What the Department Handles

  • Extreme preterm births (as early as 24 weeks gestation)
  • Neonatal sepsis — leading cause of newborn death in India
  • Respiratory Distress Syndrome requiring ventilation support
  • Birth asphyxia with therapeutic hypothermia protocol
  • Congenital anomalies requiring surgical intervention

The Gap That Your Investment Can Close

India loses over 6.5 lakh newborns every year — the highest neonatal mortality count of any country. The majority of these deaths are preventable with equipment: high-flow nasal cannulas, surfactant administration devices, transport incubators, phototherapy units, and high-frequency oscillatory ventilators. GMCH's NICU serves among the highest volumes of sick newborns in northern India, but critical equipment gaps exist.

A single corporate partner funding a 4-bed NICU expansion at GMCH would statistically prevent an estimated 120–180 neonatal deaths per year — with your company's name on the ward door.

CSR Compliance Explainer

Section 135, Companies Act 2013

India is the world's first country to mandate corporate social responsibility by law. Under Section 135 of the Companies Act 2013, companies meeting any of these thresholds must spend 2% of their average net profit over the preceding three financial years on CSR activities:

  • Net worth of ₹500 crore or more, OR
  • Turnover of ₹1,000 crore or more, OR
  • Net profit of ₹5 crore or more

Why Healthcare Qualifies

Schedule VII of the Companies Act 2013 explicitly lists "eradicating hunger, poverty and malnutrition, promoting health care including preventive health care" as a qualifying CSR activity. A contribution to GMCH is unambiguously compliant.

Scale of Opportunity

India's total CSR spend in FY 2023-24 reached ₹29,987 crore — up from ₹26,579 crore the prior year. Healthcare consistently receives the second-largest share of total CSR disbursement, with ₹22,000+ crore deployed in health initiatives across India.

Important: Avoid "Unspent CSR" Penalties

The 2021 CSR amendment mandates that unspent CSR funds be transferred to a PM National Relief Fund or other specified fund within 30 days of financial year end. Companies that fail to deploy their full CSR obligation face regulatory scrutiny. A pre-agreed partnership with GMCH, structured before year-end, ensures full deployment with zero compliance risk.
